Solution 1: Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Objective

The first approach considered was implementing Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) to strengthen authentication between the Ground Control Station (GCS) and the UAV before any communication was established.

⸻

Why We Considered 2FA

During our initial study of MAVLink, we observed that unauthorized users could potentially attempt to establish communication with the UAV if they had access to the network. To reduce this risk, we explored adding an additional authentication step before allowing mission commands or control messages.

2FA was selected because it is a widely adopted security mechanism that:

* Verifies the identity of the operator.
* Reduces the risk of unauthorized access.
* Is simple to understand and implement.
* Is commonly used in modern secure systems.

Our expectation was that authenticating the operator with a second verification factor would improve the overall security of UAV communication.

⸻

Proposed Approach

The idea was to require a second authentication factor before the Ground Control Station could communicate with the UAV. Only after successful verification would MAVLink messages be accepted for transmission.

The primary goal was to ensure that only authenticated users could issue flight commands.

⸻

Conclusion

Although 2FA successfully strengthened user authentication, further analysis revealed that it did not secure the MAVLink communication itself

Once authentication was completed, MAVLink packets continued to be transmitted in their original form without encryption or message protection

⸻

Limitations

The following limitations were identified:

* Does not encrypt MAVLink messages
* Does not provide message integrity verification
* Cannot stop replay attacks after authentication, MITM Attacks, DOS attacks
* Adds additional authentication overhead without protecting the transmitted data

⸻
Hence, we rejected it as the solution.
